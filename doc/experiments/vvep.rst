Visual Pattern Reversal VEP
===========================

Visual Evoked Potentials (VEPs) are electrical signals generated by the visual
cortex in response to visual stimulation, and reflect the integrity of the
visual pathway from the retina through to the occipital cortex.

The Pattern Reversal VEP (PR-VEP) is the most widely studied VEP paradigm. It
involves presenting a checkerboard pattern whose black and white squares swap
positions at a regular rate — typically two reversals per second — while the
participant fixates a central point. This elicits a stereotyped waveform whose
most prominent feature is the **P100**, a positive deflection occurring
approximately 100ms after each reversal.

.. warning::
   This implementation is a **research and development tool**. Its stimulus
   parameters are guided by the ISCEV clinical standard but the end-to-end
   timing accuracy has not been validated against reference hardware. It is
   **not currently suitable for clinical use or diagnosis**, and should not be
   used to make clinical decisions about individual participants. Every claim
   below about P100 latency, amplitude ranges, and diagnostic significance
   refers to the published literature on the PR-VEP paradigm in general, not
   to results obtained with this implementation specifically.


The PR-VEP Waveform
-------------------

In the published literature, the PR-VEP is characterised by a three-component
waveform recorded most prominently at midline occipital electrodes (Oz in
particular, with useful signal at O1, O2 and the parieto-occipital sites PO3,
POz, PO4):

- **N75** — a small negative deflection at ~75ms
- **P100** — a large positive deflection at ~100ms (the primary feature of interest)
- **N145** — a later negative deflection at ~135-150ms

Studies typically report absolute P100 latency, interocular P100 latency
difference, and the N75-P100 peak-to-peak amplitude.

**P100 characteristics (from the literature):**

- **Latency**: ~100ms after each reversal. Typical published normal range is
  95–115ms in healthy adults, though values depend heavily on the equipment
  and population used to establish the norms.
- **Amplitude**: Typically 3–20 μV, with substantial inter-individual variability.
- **Distribution**: Maximal at the midline occipital electrode (Oz). Because
  each retina projects to both occipital cortices after the chiasm, monocular
  full-field stimulation produces a roughly symmetric P100 across the two
  hemispheres — there is no reliable contralateral-eye dominance in this
  paradigm. (Hemifield stimulation, which this implementation does not use,
  does produce lateralised and sometimes paradoxically lateralised responses.)


Research Applications
---------------------

With this kind of caveat-laden, research-only implementation, reasonable uses
include:

- **Development and evaluation of the PR-VEP pipeline itself** — stimulus
  generation, timing validation, marker alignment, analysis scripts
- **Exploratory within-subject studies** — e.g. comparing P100 latency and
  amplitude across sessions, across eyes, or before/after a manipulation,
  where the comparison is relative rather than against a clinical norm
- **Comparative methodology studies** — e.g. comparing VR vs monitor
  presentation, checking how much fixed EEG device latency matters, or
  validating a hardware optode against the software OpenXR timestamps
- **Educational use** — understanding how an evoked-potential paradigm is
  built end to end

.. note::
   The ISCEV standard recommends testing with both a **large** (1°) and a
   **small** (15 arcmin / 0.25°) check size, because small checks are more
   sensitive to subtle timing changes in the visual pathway, while varying
   check sizes also matters for studies of visual development. **This
   implementation currently only supports the large (1°) check size** and
   monocular full-field stimulation. Studies that require small-check
   sensitivity, hemifield stimulation, or other ISCEV variants will need a
   different tool.


Stimulus Parameters
-------------------

Parameters are guided by the ISCEV standard for clinical VEPs [Odom2016]_,
though (as above) this is a research implementation and has not been validated
against the standard end-to-end:

- **Check size**: 1° of visual angle (0.5 cycles per degree) — the ISCEV
  "large" option
- **Reversal rate**: 2 reversals per second (SOA = 0.5s)
- **Field size**: 16° (monitor) / 20° (VR) — exceeds the 15° ISCEV minimum
- **Contrast**: High contrast black and white (≥80% Michelson, matching ISCEV ≥75%)
- **Mean luminance**: Held constant across reversals so the cortex responds to
  pattern change only, not to luminance change
- **Fixation**: Central red dot for stable gaze throughout each block
- **Recording**: Monocular, with blocks alternating between left and right eye
  to enable interocular P100 latency comparison


Experiment Design
-----------------

The experiment supports both standard monitor-based and VR-based (Meta Quest)
presentation, with four blocks of 50 seconds by default (configurable). Blocks
alternate between left-eye and right-eye stimulation, producing ~100 reversals
per eye per block — enough to compute clean averaged waveforms.

**Monitor mode**:

- The checkerboard is drawn centred on a black background
- Block instructions ask the participant to close one eye and fixate the red
  dot with the other
- Participants keep one eye closed manually for the duration of each 50s block

.. warning::
   In monitor mode, manual eye closure can leak light and is difficult to
   maintain for a full block without blinking or squinting. For any serious
   monocular comparison, an eye patch or occluder is strongly recommended
   over manual closure.

**VR mode (Meta Quest)**:

- Stereoscopic presentation with precise per-eye control
- The non-stimulated eye sees a solid black screen so there is no manual
  occlusion required
- A mid-grey background behind the checkerboard maintains constant mean
  luminance, preventing luminance adaptation effects
- Instructions are drawn to the active eye only; the other eye sees black

VR mode is the preferred path for this implementation because it eliminates
the monocular occlusion problem and, as described below, provides access to
sub-millisecond stimulus onset timestamps via the OpenXR compositor.


Stimulus Timing and Latency Correction
--------------------------------------

P100 latency is the most-reported feature of the PR-VEP, and accurate P100
latency requires an accurate stimulus onset timestamp. In published work on
demyelinating disease, latency shifts of only 10–20ms carry meaning, so a
fixed ~10ms of uncertainty or jitter in the stimulus marker is enough to
dominate the measurement. Most of what follows is about understanding,
reducing, and quantifying that uncertainty.

What "real-time" means here
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

An idealised real-time timestamp, for this purpose, means:

   *The marker time attached to a stimulus event represents the moment that
   stimulus was actually visible on the participant's retina, expressed in
   the same clock that the EEG samples are timestamped with.*

In practice there is no single point in software where that time is directly
observable. Instead it's the sum of several components, and "getting closer
to real-time" means measuring or cancelling each one:

1. **Output-side latency** — the delay from the moment Python calls
   ``window.flip()`` until photons actually leave the display. This includes
   the GPU render queue, driver and compositor buffering, monitor scan-out,
   LCD pixel response, and (in VR) HMD reprojection and display persistence.
   Typical magnitudes range from one frame (~16ms at 60Hz) on a direct-draw
   monitor to tens of milliseconds on some OLED TVs.
2. **Retinal and optical latency** — the few milliseconds for photons to
   excite the retina. Usually ignored because it's both small and constant.
3. **Input-side latency (EEG device delay)** — from the moment a cortical
   signal appears at the scalp until the corresponding sample is handed to
   software with its LSL timestamp. Includes electrode/amp settling, any
   anti-aliasing filter group delay, wireless buffering, and the device
   driver's sample queue. This is **device-specific and often sizeable** —
   the Muse family is typically cited at ~30–50ms, and can be larger under
   Bluetooth contention. Most of it is fixed, and can be measured once and
   subtracted as a constant offset.
4. **Clock alignment** — whether the stimulus clock (``time.time()`` on the
   presentation machine) and the EEG sample clock refer to the same
   time-base. LSL handles this automatically if you use it consistently;
   mixing ``time.time()`` with raw device timestamps can introduce tens of
   milliseconds of drift per session.

The P100 you measure is:

.. code-block:: text

   measured_P100_latency = true_P100_latency
                         + output_side_latency    (1)
                         + retinal_latency        (2, ~constant, ignored)
                         + eeg_device_latency     (3, mostly fixed)
                         + clock_drift            (4, usually 0 with LSL)

To get close to the true P100 latency, you want (1) to be measured
per-trial rather than estimated, and (3) to be characterised and
subtracted. "Realtime" is the limit in which (1) + (3) + (4) ≈ 0.

OpenXR predicted display time (VR) — how close to real-time?
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

In VR mode, each frame's EEG marker timestamp comes not from ``time()`` after
``window.flip()``, but from ``tracking_state.headPose.time`` — the OpenXR
compositor's per-frame predicted *photon time*, i.e. the moment the frame is
expected to be physically visible on the HMD displays. This is captured in
``BaseExperiment._draw()`` and stored on ``self.predicted_display_time`` for
``present_stimulus()`` to attach to the EEG marker.

What this gives you:

- It addresses component (1) — the output-side latency — and addresses it
  *per-frame*, not via a fixed lag constant. The compositor knows, for each
  frame, how long it is currently taking to submit and scan out a frame, and
  reports that as the predicted photon time. As GPU load changes, this
  number tracks it.
- The compositor uses the same predicted photon time for its own reprojection
  decisions (that's why OpenXR exposes it), so by construction it's the
  closest thing to ground truth for VR display timing that is available from
  software.
- Reported compositor-timing accuracy on the Quest Link pipeline is in the
  sub-millisecond to low-single-digit-millisecond range for a well-behaved
  session — i.e., the residual output-side error after this correction is
  much smaller than the P100 latency shifts of interest.

What it does **not** address:

- Component (3), the EEG device input latency. OpenXR knows nothing about
  the Muse; you still need to characterise device latency separately.
- Dropped frames. If the compositor misses a frame (reprojection), the
  predicted photon time for the missed frame is meaningless. This is the
  reason the implementation goes to lengths to prevent drops (see below),
  and logs them so you can detect them after the fact.
- Clock alignment if your EEG data and your stimulus markers are recorded
  under different clocks. If you use LSL consistently, this is handled.

In short: for the VR path, the output-side latency component becomes
effectively a solved problem, and the remaining uncertainty is dominated
by whatever your EEG device does.

Optode sync patch (monitor) — proof of concept, untested
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

.. warning::
   The optode sync patch is currently a **proof-of-concept implementation
   that has not yet been validated against real photodiode hardware.** Treat
   the monitor-mode timing as software-timestamp only, with unknown output-side
   latency, until hardware validation is completed.

The monitor-mode equivalent of the VR compositor timestamp would be a
hardware photodiode timing patch. A small (50px) white/black square is
drawn in the bottom-left corner of the window, and its polarity flips with
every checkerboard reversal so that a photodiode taped over that corner
produces a TTL pulse on every stimulus onset. Feeding that pulse into a
spare EEG or trigger channel would give a hardware ground truth for
stimulus timing that bypasses OS, GPU driver, and monitor latency entirely.

The code to draw the patch is in place; the hardware side (photodiode,
wiring, channel assignment, and comparison against the software markers)
has not yet been built or tested. Until it has, do not assume the
monitor-mode markers are accurate at a P100-latency-relevant level.

Do you actually need an optode?
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Probably not, most of the time. An optode is most useful:

- **Once per setup**, to measure the output-side latency of your specific
  monitor and driver stack. Do this by comparing the optode TTL pulse
  against the software marker across many reversals, compute the mean and
  variance, and check that the variance is small. If it is, you can apply
  the mean as a fixed offset during analysis and skip running the optode
  on every session.
- **For the monitor path specifically**, where there is no equivalent of
  the OpenXR predicted display time and the software marker really is the
  only thing you have. Here an optode (or a known-good monitor calibration)
  is the only way to turn the software timestamps into something latency-aware.
- **As an independent validation** of the OpenXR predicted display time if
  you want to publish results that hinge on absolute P100 latency and a
  reviewer asks how you verified the VR timing.

An optode is usually **not** worth the effort when:

- You're using the VR path and relative comparisons (within-subject,
  between-eye, between-session) where the output-side latency cancels out
  anyway.
- You only have a small channel budget — e.g. a 4-channel Muse, where
  burning one channel on the optode may cost you more in signal quality
  than it buys you in timing certainty.
- You're still in the methodology shakedown phase, where software jitter
  (dropped frames, GC pauses) is likely to dominate over any residual
  display-pipeline latency anyway.

Characterising EEG device input latency
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Component (3) above — the EEG device input latency — is the component the
OpenXR trick does **not** fix, and for a consumer-grade BLE device like
the Muse it is likely to be the largest single source of error by far.
Practical ways to handle it, in rough order of effort:

1. **Cite published device latency numbers and apply as a fixed offset.**
   Community measurements exist for the Muse family (on the order of
   30–50ms, depending on the model and driver version). Apply this as a
   constant subtraction in analysis. This is imperfect but good enough for
   within-subject and between-session comparisons, since the offset is the
   same every time.
2. **Measure it once with a simultaneous optode pulse.** If you have the
   optode hardware available, send the optode TTL simultaneously into a
   spare EEG channel and a fast reference channel (or into the stimulus
   PC directly). The difference between the pulse arrival on the EEG
   stream and the pulse arrival on the reference is the device latency.
   Do this once per device/driver combination, save the number, and apply
   it as a fixed offset.
3. **Measure it every session.** The most rigorous option, and only worth
   doing if you're publishing absolute-latency numbers against a reference
   population. Requires an optode in every recording.
4. **Work entirely in relative terms and don't bother.** Interocular
   latency differences, session-to-session changes within a participant,
   and effect sizes across a manipulation all cancel out a fixed device
   offset. For exploratory work this is the pragmatic default.

"Plan B" — normative baseline without hardware validation
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

If you want to use this implementation for within-lab exploratory work
before the timing stack has been fully validated, a reasonable plan B is:

1. **Collect your own normative baseline** by running the experiment on a
   set of healthy volunteers under your exact setup (same device, same
   refresh rate, same presentation mode, same electrode montage, same
   analysis pipeline). Record the mean and spread of P100 latency and
   interocular latency difference for that population.
2. **Fit a regression model of P100 latency on head size.** P100 latency
   depends on head size — in the published literature the effect is on
   the order of 0.1–0.2 ms per mm of head circumference or interaural
   distance, which adds 5–10ms of variation across the adult range. That
   is the same order of magnitude as the latency shifts of interest, so
   it should not be averaged over.

   The historical clinical approach to this is a binned lookup table
   (e.g. "head circumference 54–56cm: P100 98–108ms; 56–58cm: ..."). This
   is easy to hand a technician but statistically wasteful: participants
   sitting near a bin boundary get classified arbitrarily, within-bin
   variance is discarded, and adding age/sex stratification explodes the
   number of bins. **This spike branch replaces the table with a continuous
   regression model.**

   The model shape is a linear regression of P100 latency on head size,
   age, and sex:

   .. code-block:: text

      P100_latency_ms = β₀
                      + β₁ · head_size_mm
                      + β₂ · age_years
                      + β₃ · sex
                      + ε

   fit on your own normative cohort. For each new participant, the model
   gives a predicted P100 latency and a residual standard deviation,
   which lets you express "is this measurement unusual?" as a z-score
   rather than a bin lookup. See the module docstring in
   ``eegnb/analysis/vep_norms.py`` (stubbed on this branch) for the
   concrete API shape.

   Measurement recipe at baseline-collection time:

   - **Head circumference** (easiest, most common, most robust to
     measurement technique)
   - **Preauricular-to-preauricular distance** across Cz (the "interaural
     arc" — this is what 10-20 cap sizes are labelled by, so it's likely
     the number you're already measuring if you use a cap)
   - **Nasion-to-inion distance** (closest proxy for visual pathway
     length, but less commonly recorded)

   Record at least one of these as session metadata; head circumference is
   the safe default. Age and sex should also be recorded, and are
   optional terms in the fit.

   .. note::
      The regression coefficients shown in ``fit_normative_model()`` on
      this branch are placeholders. A proper fit needs a literature pass
      for plausible priors and an in-lab baseline cohort large enough to
      actually fit the model with reasonable CIs. Do not use the
      placeholder numbers for any real analysis.

   The functional form may also need revisiting. A strictly linear fit
   is a fine first pass but there are physical reasons to expect mild
   non-linearities: scalp volume scales roughly with the cube of head
   radius, and conduction velocity varies along the visual pathway.
   Before committing this out of the spike, it is worth fitting both
   a linear and a log- or square-root-transformed model and comparing
   residuals.
3. **Compare new measurements against your own baseline**, not against
   published clinical norms. This sidesteps the entire question of
   whether your absolute latency matches literature values — any fixed
   offsets in your setup get absorbed into the baseline and the residual
   is just noise and real variability.
4. **Track interocular differences** rather than absolute per-eye latency
   where possible. The interocular difference is extremely robust to
   fixed offsets *and* to head-size effects (both cancel out exactly in
   the subtraction), and is one of the more informative quantities in the
   PR-VEP literature.
5. **Repeat-measure rather than one-shot.** Running the same participant
   across multiple sessions and computing within-subject variability is
   far more diagnostic of whether your pipeline is stable than a single
   cross-sectional measurement.

This is not a substitute for proper hardware-validated timing, but it is a
practical path to getting useful exploratory data out of the implementation
in its current form.

Per-trial timing sidecar
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

Alongside every EEG recording, a companion ``{save_fn}_timing.csv`` file is
written with one row per trial and columns:

.. code-block:: text

   trial_idx, software_time, predicted_display_time, delta_ms, use_vr

where ``software_time`` is ``time.time()`` right after ``window.flip()``,
``predicted_display_time`` is the OpenXR photon time for the same frame
(``None`` on the monitor path), and ``delta_ms`` is the difference in
milliseconds. This lets you verify per-session compositor jitter after the
fact, confirm whether any fixed-lag estimates are still appropriate, and
apply per-trial timing correction during analysis if needed.

Frame-drop prevention and diagnostics
~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~

The single biggest way to wreck stimulus timing on a consumer PC is to
drop a frame. Two mechanisms reduce the chance of that happening during
a trial loop:

- **Garbage collection is disabled** during each trial loop via
  ``gc.disable()`` in a ``try/finally`` block. Python GC pauses of 1–10ms
  are a common cause of the Quest Link compositor missing its frame
  deadline (producing reprojection and the Quest "hourglass" icon). GC is
  re-enabled between blocks and after the recording ends, so no garbage
  actually leaks — it just all collects in the gaps between trial loops.
- **Elevated process priority** during trial loops via
  ``psychopy.core.rush(True)``, which bumps the process and its threads
  to a high-priority OS scheduling class. This reduces the chance that
  the trial loop is preempted by an unrelated thread or service during a
  critical flip. Priority is restored between blocks.

PsychoPy per-frame interval recording is also enabled with a 150%-of-refresh
drop threshold (so a frame that takes more than ~25ms at 60Hz counts as
dropped). After each experiment a ``{save_fn}_frame_stats.json`` sidecar
is written containing the dropped-frame count and per-frame intervals for
post-hoc analysis. If your recording has dropped frames, you know about
it and can exclude the affected trials.


Running the Experiment
----------------------

**From Python:**

.. code-block:: python

   from eegnb.devices.eeg import EEG
   from eegnb.experiments.visual_vep import VisualPatternReversalVEP

   eeg = EEG(device='muse2')  # or your device of choice
   experiment = VisualPatternReversalVEP(
       display_refresh_rate=60,   # must match your display; must be divisible by 2Hz reversal
       eeg=eeg,
       save_fn='my_vep_recording.csv',
       use_vr=True,               # False for monitor mode
   )
   experiment.run()

**Key parameters:**

- ``display_refresh_rate`` (int, required): expected display refresh rate in Hz.
  Must match the physical display, and must be divisible by the 2 Hz reversal
  rate. Common values: 60, 72, 90, 120.
- ``block_duration_seconds`` (int, default 50): duration of each block
- ``block_trial_size`` (int, default 100): reversals per block
- ``n_blocks`` (int, default 4): total blocks (alternates left/right eye)
- ``use_vr`` (bool, default False): use Meta Quest via OpenXR instead of monitor
- ``use_fullscr`` (bool, default True): fullscreen presentation

**Output files** (written alongside the EEG CSV):

- ``{save_fn}.csv`` — EEG samples with embedded stimulus markers
- ``{save_fn}_timing.csv`` — per-trial compositor timing (see above)
- ``{save_fn}_frame_stats.json`` — per-frame intervals and drop count


Electrode Placement
-------------------

The PR-VEP generators sit in the occipital cortex, so the relevant recording
sites are all at the back of the head. The midline occipital site **Oz** is
the primary electrode of interest for the P100, followed by O1, O2 and the
parieto-occipital sites PO3, POz, and PO4 if extra channels are available.

**Muse users: use the external electrode mod and place the extra electrode
at Oz.** The Muse's default dry electrodes (TP9, TP10, AF7, AF8) are all
either temporal or frontal — they will pick up a blurry, attenuated version
of the P100 but are not well placed for recovering the occipital peak. The
external-electrode mod through the microUSB port on the Muse 2 lets you add a
single extra channel, and for this experiment that extra channel is far more
valuable at Oz than at any of the default sites. POz is an acceptable
alternative if Oz is mechanically awkward with your headband/hat
arrangement, but Oz is the first choice. See the
:doc:`../misc/using_an_extra_electrode_muse` page in the Misc docs for build
and placement details.

For full-headset devices with genuine occipital coverage (OpenBCI with a
cap, g.tec Unicorn, etc.), use Oz as the primary channel and add as many of
O1/O2/PO3/POz/PO4 as your montage supports. A midline forehead site (Fpz or
Fz) works as a ground, and linked earlobes or a single mastoid as the
reference. None of this differs materially from what you'd do for any other
occipital ERP recording.
